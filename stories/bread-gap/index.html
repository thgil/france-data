The Bread Gap

Paris's 1st arrondissement has one bakery for every 480 residents. Twenty minutes away by RER, Jouy-le-Moutier has 16,500 residents and one bakery. Inside Île-de-France's baguette inequality.

2026-05-07 4 min read

Île-de-France has 6,299 boulangeries spread across 1,300 communes and 12 million residents — about 5.3 per 10,000 people on average. That sounds reasonable. But averages hide a story. The distribution of those bakeries follows a sharp gradient from central Paris outward, and at the edges it drops away almost entirely.

The data below comes from the Base Permanente des Équipements (BPE), INSEE's annual census of public and commercial services by commune. Each commune in IDF appears with its bakery count and its population. The question isn't how many bakeries there are — it's where they aren't.

Paris vs the rest

Paris the city (département 75) sits at 8.0 bakeries per 10,000 residents — well above every surrounding département. Seine-Saint-Denis (93), the dense inner-ring suburb to the north-east, comes closest at 5.2/10k. Further out, Essonne (91) and Val-d'Oise (95) fall to 4.3–4.4. That gap looks modest in a bar chart. In lived experience, it's the difference between a boulangerie on your street and a ten-minute drive.

Inside Paris, the gradient continues

Paris itself is not uniform. The historic core — 1st, 4th, 6th, 8th, 9th arrondissements — clusters around 13–21 bakeries per 10,000 residents. Paris 1er leads at 20.8/10k: 36 boulangeries for 17,300 people, or roughly one per 480 residents. These are the arrondissements where the tourist walks past three boulangeries before deciding which queue to join.

Beyond Paris, a few well-served small towns appear in the top 15. Rebais, a market town of 2,150 in Seine-et-Marne, ranks first overall at 23.2/10k — but with just 5 bakeries, it's a statistical quirk. Among communes with more than 10,000 residents, the Paris arrondissements hold every top spot.

Nearly half of IDF communes have no bakery at all

The most striking number isn't Paris's surplus — it's the periphery's absence. 607 of 1,300 IDF communes (46.7%) have zero registered bakeries. Not one. In Seine-et-Marne, the region's largest département by area and the one furthest from central Paris, 301 of 514 communes — 59% — are bakery-free.

Many of these zero-bakery communes are small: a few hundred residents, no commercial centre, bread bought in the next town over or at the supermarket. But not all of them are small. Forty-six communes with populations above 3,000 have zero bakeries. Two top 5,000.

Urban towns, nearly bread-free

The hardest cases to explain away are the towns large enough to have a mairie, a school, a pharmacy — but not a boulangerie. Jouy-le-Moutier in Val-d'Oise has 16,589 residents and one bakery: 0.6 per 10,000, compared to Paris 1er's 20.8. Carrières-sur-Seine in Yvelines has 15,473 residents and two bakeries. These are postwar suburbs built around cars and supermarkets, where the grande surface absorbed bread-buying decades ago. The boulangerie, if it survived at all, became a single outlier.

What the map of bread tells you about the map of everything

The boulangerie was never purely commercial. It's infrastructure — the morning anchor, the reason to walk, the thing that makes a quartier feel like one. Its distribution across IDF maps almost perfectly onto the broader geography of urban investment: dense in the historic core, thinning fast at the périphérique, absent across wide swaths of outer Seine-et-Marne and Val-d'Oise.

The 607 zero-bakery communes are a different kind of map. They're not necessarily poor or failing — many are comfortable commuter towns. But they've traded the neighbourhood bakery for something else: a car, a supermarket aisle, a frozen baguette. The tradition hasn't died; it's just concentrated. The bread is still there. It's just that most of it is in Paris.

A note on the data. Bakery counts and commune populations come from INSEE's Base Permanente des Équipements (BPE) 2023, preprocessed for IDF by this project's earlier pharmacy-myth story. The dataset covers 1,300 communes in Île-de-France. "Bakery" (boulangerie/pâtisserie) corresponds to the BPE equipment type D201. Paris is treated as 20 arrondissements (communes) rather than one unit, which gives the granular per-arrondissement breakdown.
