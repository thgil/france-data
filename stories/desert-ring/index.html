The desert at the gate

France has 11 million people living in medical deserts. A quarter of them live in the suburbs of Paris.

May 2026 5 min read Data: DREES APL 2023

France's medical deserts are well-documented. The APL score — accessibilité potentielle localisée, a measure of how many GP consultations a resident can reasonably access per year — shows vast swathes of the country below the official 2.5 threshold. Map it and you see the diagonale du vide: that half-empty diagonal running from the Meuse to the Pyrenees where population has been draining for a century. Lozère, Creuse, Haute-Marne — names that feel remote. The image that comes to mind is a village without a doctor, a drive of an hour to the nearest surgery.

That image is not wrong. But it is incomplete. Sort France's 96 metropolitan départements not by the share of communes that are deserts, but by the raw number of people living in them — and the top four are not in the Massif Central. They are the suburbs of Paris.

The ranking nobody expected

Val-d'Oise (95): 793,798 people in medical deserts. Seine-et-Marne (77): 792,589. Essonne (91): 673,703. Yvelines (78): 563,020. The four outer Île-de-France départements account for 2.82 million desert residents — more than a quarter of the national total — and they do so from a combined population of 5.46 million.

The rural heavyweights — Lozère, Creuse, Haute-Marne — appear lower down the absolute ranking because, however dire their access scores, they simply do not have many people. Lozère (48) has 32,302 residents in medical deserts. Val-d'Oise has 793,798: that is 24 times more. Cher (18), at 60.5% of its population in deserts, has a higher percentage than Val-d'Oise — but 612,000 fewer desert residents. The percentage map shows one story. The headcount tells another.

The gradient through the périphérique

Paris (département 75) has an average APL of 4.96. Not a single one of its 2.13 million residents lives in a medical desert. The city is — by this measure — almost perfectly served. The GP network is dense, the population walkably concentrated, the public transport irrelevant because you can usually walk.

Cross the boulevard périphérique and the picture degrades by ring.

Département Desert residents % of population

Hauts-de-Seine (92) and Val-de-Marne (94), the inner suburbs immediately outside Paris, sit between 12% and 17%. They inherit some of the capital's density and GP concentration. Seine-Saint-Denis (93), one of France's poorest départements, is at 12.9% — partially buffered by its urban concentration, though that figure masks severe access gaps within the département.

Then comes the outer ring. Yvelines (78): 38.7%, 563,020 people. Essonne (91): 51.3%, 673,703 people. Seine-et-Marne (77): 55.1%, 792,589 people. Val-d'Oise (95): 63.2%, 793,798 people. These are not peripheral regions of France. They are reachable from central Paris in 30–60 minutes by RER or train. The territory exists on every commuter map. The healthcare infrastructure does not.

Why the suburbs, not the countryside?

The APL model measures access to GPs using a catchment-area calculation that accounts for both supply (number of doctors, weighted by activity) and demand (resident population, weighted by age). Rural deserts score low because there are very few doctors relative to the area. Suburban deserts score low for a different reason: population grew fast over the postwar decades — the grands ensembles, the pavillonnaires, the villes nouvelles — and the healthcare infrastructure never kept pace.

Paris's GP network was laid down over centuries, aligned with the street plan, the pharmacy grid, the old quartier logic. The outer ring was built in decades, aligned with the RER, the motorway junction, the employment zone. The baker and the pharmacist sometimes followed. The GP, who needed a patient base that took years to build, less often did.

The diagonale du vide gets the headlines. It is real, photogenic, and confirms what urban France has always believed about rural France. The ring around Paris is less visible — it looks, on the surface, like suburban France, not like a desert. The APL data says otherwise: 2.82 million people, 51.7% of the outer ring's population, living below the official threshold. The desert is not always far away.

Data. APL scores are from DREES (Direction de la recherche, des études, de l'évaluation et des statistiques), 2023 vintage, as used in the medical-deserts story. Département-level figures are aggregated from 35,014 commune records in the DREES APL file joined to administrative boundaries. Overseas territories (971–976) are excluded. Population figures from INSEE 2021 base included in the APL dataset. The desert threshold is APL < 2.5, the official DREES classification for sous-densité médicale.
