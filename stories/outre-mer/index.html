Five Frances

France's overseas departments span four oceans and 8,000 kilometres. Their healthcare access spans from 0% medical desert to 68%. La Réunion leads every metropolitan department. Guyane has nine communes where the official GP access score is zero.

2026-05-07 5 min read

France measures GP accessibility using a score called the APL — Accessibilité Potentielle Localisée. Below 2.5, a commune is officially a medical desert. The score covers every commune in France, including the four overseas departments that appear in the DREES dataset: Guadeloupe, Martinique, Guyane, and La Réunion. Mayotte, France's fifth overseas department, is absent.

When you look at those four territories side by side, the differences are so stark they seem like they must be measuring different countries.

La Réunion: the exception

Réunion is a volcanic island of 2,512 km² in the Indian Ocean, 9,300 km from Paris. It has 24 communes. Not one of them is a medical desert. The lowest APL score on the island is 2.64, in Entre-Deux, a small town tucked between two volcanic calderas. The national threshold is 2.5.

The average APL across Réunion's 24 communes is 4.80 — higher than Paris (4.96 in the capital, with much higher variance), better than Lyon, Bordeaux, or any metropolitan department outside the very best. Saint-Louis, in the south, reaches 8.71: more than three times the threshold, equivalent to some of the ski-resort communes that top the national rankings. For 871,000 residents spread across a tropical island, that is an extraordinary achievement.

Why does Réunion perform so well? The island is compact and relatively dense. Its population is concentrated along a coastal ring road, with most towns within 30–40 minutes of a city centre. There is no diagonale du vide, no historical demographic haemorrhage depopulating the interior. The interior highlands are sparsely populated; the coast, where almost everyone lives, is well-served.

Guyane: the other end

French Guyane is also an overseas department. It shares a currency and a state with Réunion. It is otherwise barely comparable. Guyane covers 83,534 km² of Amazonian rainforest — roughly the size of Austria — with 22 communes spread across territory that has almost no road network outside its coastal strip. You reach the interior by pirogue along jungle rivers, or by small aircraft.

Of Guyane's 286,618 residents, 195,876 — 68.3% — live in medical deserts. Nine communes record an APL of exactly zero: no accessible GP consultations per resident per year, not because the metric rounds down but because there is, on the official measure, no supply.

Kourou launches Europe's rockets. Its GP access score is 2.28 — a medical desert.

The interior communities are the most extreme cases. Maripasoula (9,177 people), Grand-Santi (9,120), and Papaichton (5,606) sit on the Maroni River near the Suriname border, reachable only by water or air. Their APL: 0.00.

But the coastal towns are also struggling. Saint-Laurent-du-Maroni is Guyane's second-largest city, with 50,250 residents. Its APL is 1.59 — firmly in medical desert territory. Matoury, the third-largest, has 34,810 people and an APL of 2.22. Kourou, population 24,612, is the commune where the Centre Spatial Guyanais operates — where Europe launches its Ariane, Soyuz, and Vega rockets into orbit. Kourou's APL is 2.28. By France's own measure, the town that lifts satellites into space cannot reliably provide its residents a GP appointment.

The Caribbean middle

Martinique and Guadeloupe sit between the two extremes. Martinique's 34 communes are home to 360,749 people; 6.8% live in medical deserts, better than the metropolitan average. Fort-de-France, the capital, has an APL of 4.70. The exception is Grand'Rivière, a small fishing village at Martinique's northernmost tip — isolated by terrain, its APL is 0.35, the lowest on the island and severe by any measure.

Guadeloupe's picture is close to the metropolitan average: 16% of its 384,315 residents are in medical deserts. The outlier is La Désirade, a long narrow island east of the main archipelago, home to 1,391 people and an APL of zero. It is one of the most remote inhabited French communes, accessible by ferry from Saint-François — and it records the same score as the most isolated Amazonian villages.

Mayotte: the blank

Mayotte became France's 101st department in 2011. It does not appear in the DREES APL dataset. There is no row for Dzaoudzi, for Mamoudzou, for any of the 17 communes of this small island in the Comoros archipelago. France's youngest, poorest, and most densely populated department — with a poverty rate above 70% and a population of roughly 320,000 — is simply absent from the official measure of healthcare access.

The absence is not an oversight; it reflects genuine data collection challenges in a territory where census figures are contested and healthcare infrastructure is patchy at best. But it means that when France maps its medical deserts, one of its most acute healthcare crises is invisible.

The pattern

Réunion's success and Guyane's crisis both follow geography. Réunion is small, dense, and coherently connected. Guyane is vast, sparse, and physically fragmented. Martinique and Guadeloupe are mid-sized Caribbean islands with real urban centres — their healthcare access broadly reflects that structure.

The lesson from overseas France is the same lesson the metropolitan medical desert map teaches about the diagonale du vide: healthcare access in France tracks density and connectivity, not administrative status. Being French, officially, does not mean the doctors are near.
