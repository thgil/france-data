Y, Oô, and 35,226 Others

One letter. Forty-five characters. The shape of France's 35,228 commune names — and a few surprises about which saints actually dominate.

2026-05-05 5 min read

France has 35,228 communes — more than Germany (11,000), Italy (7,900), and Spain (8,100) combined. The number is famous. Less discussed is what it implies for names: 35,228 of them, each official, each encoded in the Code officiel géographique (COG) maintained by INSEE. Most are unremarkable compound words or saints' dedications. A small number are genuinely strange.

The names are linguistic sediment. Gaulish roots, Latin church dedications, Norman place-words, revolutionary renaming, the occasional administrative accident. Every fusion and fission of communes since the Revolution has produced a new name — sometimes by concatenating two old ones with a hyphen, sometimes by choosing one, sometimes by coining something entirely new. The result is a corpus that rewards examination.

The shortest

The shortest commune name in France is a single letter. Y, in the Somme (département 80), code 80829. The name is not an accident or abbreviation — it derives from a Gaulish root for water (eo, cognate with the Yères river and similar hydronyms across northern France). The village's sign-painter presumably had an easy assignment.

Below Y in length sit 15 two-character communes. They range from the Norman Eu (Seine-Maritime, code 76255 — once a medieval county and the site of Louis-Philippe's meetings with Queen Victoria) to the Alpine Oz (Isère, known today as a ski station) to the Pyrenean Ur and Py (both Pyrénées-Orientales, Catalan in origin). Several carry circumflex accents that hint at lost vowels: Bô, Bû, Oô.

The most common

If you had to guess the most common exact commune name in France, you might reach for Saint-Martin — the most widely distributed church dedication in the country, the patron of soldiers and wine-growers. You'd be wrong. Sainte-Colombe leads the list with 12 communes sharing the name exactly, spread across 12 different départements from Lot to Ille-et-Vilaine. Saint-Sauveur (11) and Saint-Aubin (10) follow. Saint-Martin as an exact, standalone name does not appear in the top 25 at all: most "Saint-Martin" communes carry a suffix that makes them unique — Saint-Martin-de-Bréhal, Saint-Martin-sur-Nohain, and so on.

The pattern across the top 25 is striking: saints outnumber every other category. Two secular names break through — Pin (10, the pine tree) and Beaulieu (10, beautiful place) — but otherwise the list reads like a hagiology. The saints in question aren't the famous ones; they're the regional and obscure dedications, each common enough in the medieval church's geography to have been applied independently by a dozen villages.

The saints

Zoom out from individual names and the saint-naming pattern becomes unmistakable. Exactly 3,980 communes — more than 1 in 9 — begin with Saint or Sainte. The breakdown: 3,668 start with Saint-, 294 with Sainte-, and 2 with Saints-. If you count communes where a saint's name appears anywhere in the name (not just as a prefix), the figure rises to 4,520.

The legal mechanism behind this density is medieval: communes typically carry the dedication of their parish church, which was assigned by the diocese at a time when the church calendar was the most practical way to coordinate naming across hundreds of scattered villages with no radio and no internet. The system was efficient and has simply never been untangled. A 1793 revolutionary decree tried to de-Christianise commune names by replacing saints with secular descriptions — the Somme's Saint-Just becoming Brutus-sur-Noye for a few years — but most reverted to their saints after 1795.

The connectors

French commune names encode real geography in their prepositions. 1,996 communes are "X-sur-Y" — X sitting above or on the river or hill Y. Nearly every one of them genuinely sits beside a river named Y. The sur connector is the most precise: Pouilly-sur-Loire sits on the Loire, Châtillon-sur-Seine on the Seine, Pont-sur-Yonne on the Yonne. This is an accidental spatial database embedded in the naming system.

The en connector indicates that a commune is nested inside a natural or historical territory: Saint-Remy-en-Champagne (in the Champagne region), Vitry-en-Artois (in the Artois plain). The sous connector has a more literal geography: sous (under) typically means the commune lies at the foot of a feature, often a castle or hill: Savigny-sous-Mâlain, Thil-sous-Vaudrey. The prepositions are a navigational system from a world without GPS.

The longest

At the far end of the distribution sits the longest commune name in metropolitan France:

Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont-Saint-Genest-et-Isson, in Marne (département 51), stretches to 45 characters. It was created in 1972 when the communes of Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont, Saint-Genest-et-Isson merged and both parties refused to lose their name. The solution — concatenate them all — produces something that would overflow most web form fields and require a custom sign order from the mairie. The runner-up, Beaujeu-Saint-Vallier-Pierrejux-et-Quitteur in Haute-Saône (43 characters), is another merger product from the same era.

Haute-Saône (département 70) appears twice in the top five longest names — an artefact of the 1970s commune merger programme, which was more actively pursued in some regions than others. The department also happens to be home to Gy (2 characters), one of the shortest. The range within a single département: 2 to 43 characters.

The distribution

Between "Y" and the 45-character merger names lies a wide curve. The average commune name is 11.6 characters; the median is 10. The peak of the distribution falls at 7 characters — names like Avignon, Orléans, Limoges in length, though most 7-character communes are not major cities. There is a distinct shoulder between 14 and 20 characters, produced by the large number of hyphenated "Saint-X-de-Y" compounds where the full name carries both the saint and the geography.

The long tail beyond 30 characters is thin but real: 10 communes exceed 30 characters. These are exclusively the products of forced mergers — administrative convenience stitched into geography, preserved in a name that no one particularly loves but no one has got round to shortening.

A note on the data. Name counts are drawn from the gregoiredavid/france-geojson repository (itself sourced from INSEE's Code officiel géographique). The vintage is approximately 2022; communes that have merged or separated since will differ slightly. The total of 35,228 reflects metropolitan France only (excluding overseas départements and collectivités). Commune counts shift each year as mergers and separations occur — INSEE's COG is updated annually each January 1.

The "most common name" analysis counts exact string matches on the nom field of the COG. Accents are preserved (Sainte-Colombe and Sainte-Colombe are the same; Sainte-Colombe and Saint-Colombe would be counted separately if both existed). Communes named "Saint-Martin-de-X" are not counted under "Saint-Martin" — only exact matches. This is why the famous Saint-Martin does not appear in the top 25.
