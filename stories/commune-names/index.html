The naming of France

35,014 communes, one letter to 45 letters long. One in nine starts with "Saint." And then there is Y.

6 May 2026 4 min read Data: INSEE Code Officiel Géographique via DREES APL 2023

France is famously a country of villages. The Revolution created the commune in 1789 as the basic unit of the republic — and the republic created a lot of them. Today there are 35,014, down from nearly 38,000 in the nineteenth century thanks to mergers, but still extraordinary by European standards. Germany, four times the area, has roughly 11,000 municipalities. France has three times as many.

Each one has a name. What those names reveal about French history, geography, and the medieval Catholic church is the subject of this story.

Y

The shortest commune name in France is a single letter: Y. It sits in the Somme, département 80, a few kilometres south of Amiens. Population: around ninety. It was not named by someone lazy. The name derives from eau — the Old French and Picard word for water — which in the local dialect compressed over centuries to a single vowel. The village sits near a marsh. It makes sense. Postal workers have been confused for generations.

Thirteen French communes have two-letter names: Sy, By, Bû, Oô, Oz, Ri, Uz, Py, Ur, Gy, Eu, Ry, Us. Most are in Normandy, Franche-Comté, or the Pyrenees, where Occitan and Gascon roots compressed place names into vowel clusters. Oô (Haute-Garonne) is the one that sounds like you've just stubbed your toe.

The saint problem

One in every nine French communes — 3,891 in total, or 11.1% — begins with Saint- or Sainte-. This is not a coincidence. Medieval parish churches gave their names to the settlements that grew up around them, and those churches were dedicated to saints. When the commune system absorbed the existing parish geography in 1789, the saint names came with it.

Which saint won? By a comfortable margin, Saint-Martin. Two hundred and two communes carry that prefix. Martin of Tours was a fourth-century Roman soldier who allegedly sliced his military cloak in two to share it with a beggar outside Amiens — not far from Y, incidentally — and later became a bishop and one of the first non-martyr saints of the Western church. He is also the patron saint of France. The numbers reflect that.

Saint-Martin beats Saint-Jean by 202 to 155. Saint-Pierre finishes third. France's most popular saint is also, definitively, its most popular address.

The most common exact commune name — Saint-something with no disambiguation — is Sainte-Colombe, which appears twelve times. There are twelve Sainte-Colombes scattered across France, in departments from Gironde to Haute-Savoie. Colombe means dove; the name refers to a third-century martyr from Sens. When the author Georges Simenon set a Maigret mystery in Sainte-Colombe, he presumably picked it because nobody could pin him down on which one.

The prepositions of France

French communes love a geographic preposition. 1,991 communes have -sur- in their name — literally "on," meaning built beside a river. Another 974 use -en- ("in," meaning set within a forest or valley), and 257 use -sous- ("under," usually meaning at the foot of a hill or castle). Together that's nearly 3,200 communes that carry their landscape in their name.

The prepositions are a geography lesson. -sur-Mer (on the sea) clusters on the Channel and Atlantic coasts. -sur-Seine and -sur-Loire trace the great rivers. -en-Provence, -en-Bourgogne, -en-Bretagne mark regional affiliations that predate modern administrative boundaries by centuries.

How long is a commune name?

The average commune name is 11.8 characters. The distribution peaks sharply at seven and eight characters, covers a long right tail, and ends with a single outlier at 45.

That right tail is produced by communes nouvelles — mergers. When two or three villages merge into a single administrative unit, they often keep all their old names joined by hyphens and et. The result can be magnificent. The longest commune name in France, at 45 characters, is:

Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont-Saint-Genest-et-Isson

It is in the Marne, département 51. It was created in 1972 by the merger of Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont, Saint-Genest, and Isson. Population: a few hundred. It is not especially well-served by the postal system.

89.4% uniquely named

Despite 3,891 saint communes and 202 Saint-Martins, the overwhelming majority of French communes have a name shared by nobody else: 31,314 of 35,014, or 89.4%, are uniquely named. The remaining 10.6% — 3,700 communes — share their name with at least one other commune somewhere in France.

That's a surprisingly high uniqueness rate given the constraints. The Catholic canon of popular saints is not large. The geographical prepositions are only a handful. And yet French communes managed to differentiate themselves well enough that nine in ten are unambiguous on a map — the postal system's silent achievement, sustained over two and a half centuries.
