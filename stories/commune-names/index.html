The naming of France

35,014 communes, one named Y, twelve named Sainte-Colombe, and 3,891 that begin with Saint. A portrait of France through its place names.

6 May 2026 5 min read

France is a nation of 35,014 communes. Its smallest have fewer than a dozen inhabitants; its largest is Paris with 2.1 million. But what they all share — what the republic requires — is a name. Together those names span a millennium of history: the Gaulish syllables that escaped the Roman conquest, the Christian saints that Norman bishops planted across every valley, the compound bureaucratic fusions that followed the Revolution. They are the most compressed archive of French civilisation there is.

And one of them is a single letter.

Y
Commune · Somme · Population 89

Y is a real commune in the Somme, département 80, population 89. Its name comes from an old Picard word for a bend in a river. It has been called Y since at least the 12th century, long before anyone worried about the minimum length of a legal place name. It is the shortest commune name in France — and one of the shortest official place names in the world.

Y is not alone in brevity. France has a two-letter club: thirteen communes whose names are exactly two characters long. They cluster in the north, the Jura, the Pyrénées — the zones where pre-Latin roots survived longest.

These are not abbreviations. They are whole names, ancient syllables that have simply refused to grow. Eu (Seine-Maritime, pop. 6,591) is the largest of them — a proper town with a château that once hosted Victor Hugo. Oô (Haute-Garonne, pop. 103) is the strangest to spell. Uz (Hautes-Pyrénées, pop. 36) is the smallest.

The Saint problem

At the other extreme of France's naming imagination sits the saint. France has 3,891 communes — roughly one in nine — whose names begin with "Saint" or "Sainte". The republic is officially secular, but its map is profoundly otherwise.

3,891
Communes beginning with Saint or Sainte — 11.1% of all communes

The most common first word in all of France's commune names is "Saint", appearing 3,596 times. The next-closest is "La" at 1,054. France didn't just name its villages after saints; it named them so consistently that sainthood became the default mode of French topography.

The Sainte-Colombe problem

The most duplicated commune name in France is Sainte-Colombe. There are twelve of them. They stretch from the Hautes-Alpes (pop. 59) to Seine-et-Marne (pop. 1,793). If you address a letter to "Sainte-Colombe, France" without a département code, French postal services must choose between twelve possible destinations.

The next-most-common names are Saint-Sauveur (11 communes) and Saint-Aubin and Beaulieu (10 each). In total, 1,450 names are shared by at least two communes — meaning more than 4% of all France's commune names are not unique identifiers. The five-digit INSEE commune code is not a bureaucratic formality. It is often the only way to know which commune you mean.

The hyphenated republic

France has a long history of merging villages while insisting that neither name disappear. The result is that 13,410 communes — 38.3% of the total — contain at least one hyphen. These are the compound names, the administrative fossils of two or three or four villages that were once distinct, then fused, and then hyphenated into a single legal entity.

The logical endpoint of this process is the longest commune name in France:

Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont-Saint-Genest-et-Isson
Marne (51) · Population 491 · 45 characters

This commune in the Marne has 491 residents and a name that takes longer to type than the average tweet takes to write. It was formed by the merger of three historic communes — Saint-Remy-en-Bouzemont, Saint-Genest, and Isson — in 1972. Rather than choose, the municipality hyphenated all three. The commune's postal abbreviation is almost never used; people say "Saint-Remy" and mean the whole thing.

A nation in histogram form

Plot all 35,014 commune names by their character count and a clear pattern emerges. Most names cluster between 5 and 10 characters — traditional names of Gaulish, Latin, or Frankish origin that were short from the start. But there is a distinct second hump between 15 and 25 characters, the compound names created by the waves of mergers that followed the Revolution and continued into the 20th century.

The average commune name is 11.8 characters long — which feels right. It is just long enough to be distinctive, just short enough to fit on a signpost.

Named for water

France is a country of rivers, and its commune names reflect that. A full 1,991 communes — 5.7% — contain the particle "-sur-", the French shorthand for "on the [river]". These are the river towns: Nantes-sur-Loire (not its actual name, but the convention), the Neuville-sur-Saône, the Blanquefort-sur-Briolance. They cluster on the Seine, the Loire, the Rhône, the Garonne — the arteries of France long before the railways arrived.
