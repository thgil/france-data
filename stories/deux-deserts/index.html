Two deserts

Lozère has twice as many bars per capita as Paris. It also has almost no doctors. The outer IDF suburbs have neither. France's two deserts are not the same places.

2026-05-06 5 min read

When france-data mapped the medical deserts last year, 12,771 communes showed up below the APL 2.5 threshold. The same story noted that the diagonale du vide — the empty diagonal running from the Meuse to the Pyrenees — appeared in bar density too. The implication was clear: the same depopulated places are losing both their doctors and their bars.

That assumption is wrong. Or rather, only half right.

France has 49,385 registered bars and 35,014 communes, 36.5% of which are below the official medical access threshold. When you map bar density against medical desert status by département, two very different clusters emerge — and they tell two very different stories about what "abandonment" means in France.

Bars vs. doctors: no correlation

Plot every metropolitan département — bars per 10,000 residents on the horizontal axis, share of medical-desert communes on the vertical — and what you get is a cloud with no slope. There is essentially no correlation between how many bars a département has per capita and how medically underserved it is.

Two clusters are visible once you look carefully. In the top-left — high medical desert, low bars — sits a tight group of orange dots: the outer ring of Île-de-France. In the top-right — high medical desert, high bars — sit the rural departments of Corsica, the Massif Central, and Bretagne. In the bottom-right, low desert, high bars, sits a single red dot: Paris.

The suburbs that lost everything

Four outer IDF departments rank at the very bottom of France for bar density, while simultaneously topping the charts for medical deserts:

Essonne's 3.1 bars per 10,000 residents is the lowest of any metropolitan département in France. Yvelines and Hauts-de-Seine follow at 3.3. To put that in perspective: the average French village, the stereotype of rural emptiness, has more bars per resident than the commuter suburbs of the capital.

The explanation is structural. The outer IDF was built for cars, not for streets. It grew in the era of the pavillon — the detached suburban house — and the ZAC, the zoned commercial area with its supermarché and its fast-food strip. The café was never part of the plan. Neither was the cabinet médical.

Seine-et-Marne: 72.6% of its communes are medical deserts. 3.8 bars per 10,000 residents. More people live there than in Lyon, and it scores worse than most of rural France on both counts.

Rural France: missing doctors, kept the bistrots

Now look at the other end of the bar-density axis. Haute-Corse tops the chart at 21.1 bars per 10,000 residents — four times Essonne's rate. Lozère is second at 17.6, Corse-du-Sud third at 16.9. These departments have more bars per capita than Copenhagen, Berlin, or New York.

They are also, without exception, heavily medical deserts. Lozère: 61.8% of communes below APL 2.5. Haute-Corse: 64.4%. Cantal: 40.4%. The doctors left; the bars didn't.

This is not a coincidence. The same forces that drove doctors out of rural France — low income density, a patient population too scattered to fill a waiting room — do not apply to bars. A bistrot can survive on one village's worth of regulars. A GP practice needs critical mass. The thresholds are different.

There is something else too. Rural French café culture is old, baked in, maintained by inertia and by the absence of alternatives. In Seine-et-Marne, there was never a culture of the neighbourhood café to lose; the suburb was built after it had stopped being built. The architectural memory isn't there.

Paris: the only place that has both

Paris sits alone in the bottom-right of the scatter: zero medical-desert communes, an average APL of 5.41 (against a national average below 3), and 14.6 bars per 10,000 — among the highest in France, beaten only by Corsica and the rural Massif Central departments that have bars but no one to serve.

The disparity between Paris and its immediate suburbs is the sharpest geographic contrast in the French healthcare system. The gap between Paris (APL 5.41) and adjacent Seine-et-Marne (APL 2.11) is larger than the gap between the best and worst-served departments in most other European countries.

On the bar metric, the contrast is equally stark. Paris at 14.6 bars per 10,000; Essonne next door at 3.1. You could drive twenty minutes south on the A6 and the density drops by a factor of five.

Two types of abandonment

France gets described as a country with a problem of déserts médicaux, and this is true. But the medical desert map and the bar desert map are measuring different kinds of absence, in different places, for different reasons.

Rural France — the diagonale du vide, the Massif Central, interior Bretagne — lost its doctors because the economics of primary care don't work at low population density. It kept its bars because bars run on social logic, not economic logic. These places are medically underserved. They are not, in any meaningful social sense, empty.

The outer IDF suburbs — Essonne, Yvelines, Val-d'Oise, Seine-et-Marne — lost the doctors because the same low-density suburban sprawl that makes primary care economically marginal also made the density of bar customers too thin to sustain a café. The suburb was built without either. It is medically and socially underserved.

These are not the same desert. They need different maps.
