A republic of villages

France has nearly 35,000 communes — more than Germany, Italy, Spain, and Poland combined. Half of them have fewer than 500 people. Here's what that actually looks like.

2026-05-06 4 min read

In 1789, the Revolutionary Assemblée nationale needed a way to organize the new French Republic. They solved it by turning every church parish into a municipality. France had roughly 44,000 parishes. Some merged over the centuries; a few thousand were created anew. Today, France has approximately 34,800 communes in metropolitan territory and the overseas départements.

Germany, with a similar population, has around 11,000 municipalities. Italy: 7,900. Spain: 8,100. Poland: 2,500. France, alone, has more municipalities than all four of those countries combined — and has spent 230 years debating what to do about it.

The median French commune has 456 residents. That's one village square, a mairie open two mornings a week, a church with a leaking roof, and a tabac that probably closed in 2012. Half of France's communes are smaller than this.

The distribution is extreme. Chart the communes by size and what you get is a graph that falls off a cliff — a huge mass of tiny places on one end and a thin tail of cities on the other.

52.6% of French communes have fewer than 500 residents. Together they hold 6.3% of the population. The other 93.7% live in the remaining 47.4% of communes.

The paradox runs the other way too. France has 48 communes with more than 100,000 residents — cities like Toulouse, Nice, Nantes, Montpellier. These 48 communes, out of 34,788, contain 12.6% of the entire French population.

The municipal map and the population map are, in a meaningful sense, two different Frances. One is a patchwork of 35,000 villages. The other is a country where most people live in a few dozen urban agglomerations, surrounded by a vast hinterland of nearly-empty administrative units.

The emptiness isn't evenly spread. Some départements are overwhelmingly composed of tiny communes. In Haute-Marne — one of the great emptying-out stories of post-war France — 64.6% of the département's 426 communes have fewer than 200 residents. Meuse, its neighbour to the north-east, is at 60.7%.

These are the same départements that show up in the medical desert maps, in the bar desert maps, in every depopulation story about the diagonale du vide. The empty diagonal that runs from the Meuse to the Pyrenees isn't just a metaphor — it's a structural reality, visible from the population data of every single commune.

Six communes where nobody lives

Scattered through the data are communes with zero recorded residents. Many of these are technical artefacts — a recently-merged commune whose parent code now carries no population — but six of them are different. They sit in the Meuse, in the shadow of Verdun.

Between February and December 1916, the Battle of Verdun destroyed several villages completely. The French government chose not to rebuild them. Instead, it maintained them as official communes: each has a mayor (appointed rather than elected), a municipal council, an annual budget drawn partly from government subsidy. They have addresses, postal codes, and no inhabitants. They have been formally uninhabited for over a century.

The names tell the story: Mort-Homme — Dead Man's Hill. These are not just communes without people; they are places that France decided to keep on the map, indefinitely, as the administrative equivalent of a cenotaph.

France has been trying to rationalize its commune structure since at least the 1970s. The loi Marcellin (1971) encouraged voluntary mergers; the loi RCT (2010) created communes nouvelles to make fusion easier. Progress has been slow. The number of communes has dropped from around 38,000 in 1960 to roughly 34,800 today — a reduction of about 9% in sixty years.

The resistance is cultural as much as administrative. French communes are where local identity lives. The mairie is the physical expression of the Republic in places that see a préfet perhaps once a decade. Merging Fleury-devant-Douaumont with a neighbouring village would save money; it would also erase something. France, on the whole, has decided the erasure costs more than the saving.

Sources: Population data from the DREES APL 2023 commune dataset (population base INSEE 2021), covering 34,788 communes in metropolitan France and the overseas départements. The three city codes for Paris (75056), Lyon (69123), and Marseille (13055) are excluded — these cities are subdivided into numbered arrondissements, each counted as a separate commune, so the parent code carries zero population. Overseas collectivités (code prefix 98) are excluded as their population data is not included in the DREES dataset. The "ghost communes" are those with zero recorded residents in the Meuse département (55) — the six WWI villages morts pour la France around Verdun. Population figures for these six communes are officially 0; some sources list one or two inhabitants (caretakers); none have had a regular resident population since 1918.
