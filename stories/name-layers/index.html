Five tongues, 35,014 names

The commune names of France are linguistic fossils — Alsatian, Breton, Occitan, Frankish, Norse — each layer frozen in the map since the Middle Ages.

2026-05-07 6 min read

France has 35,014 communes — more than any other country in Europe. Each one has an official name, registered in the Code officiel géographique maintained by INSEE. Most names look unremarkable until you ask what language they're in. The answer, for several thousand of them, is not French.

Place names are the most conservative objects in a language. Rivers get their names from the first people to live alongside them and rarely change again. Villages take their names from Roman landowners, Frankish chieftains, Celtic holy springs, and Norse navigational markers — and keep them for fifteen centuries, through revolutions, annexations, and five republics. You can read France's history from its map, one suffix at a time.

Layer 1 — The Germanic East

-heim, -willer, -bach: Alsace's German names

373 communes
94% in Bas-Rhin + Haut-Rhin

Ribeauvillé, Obernai, Kientzheim, Kaysersberg — the communes of Alsace carry names that are unmistakably German. The suffixes -heim (home, settlement), -willer (farmstead, from Germanic weiler), and -bach (stream) make up 373 commune names, of which 94% are concentrated in just two departments: Bas-Rhin (63%) and Haut-Rhin (31%).

The concentration is the most geographically extreme of any linguistic pattern in France. Alsace changed hands between France and the Holy Roman Empire in 1648, then between France and Germany in 1871, 1918, 1940, and 1945. The borders moved repeatedly; the names never did. -heim communes remain in their medieval positions, indifferent to the treaties signed over them.

The pattern spills slightly into neighbouring Moselle — 20 communes, 5% of the total — where Frankish and Rhenish German influence overlapped. Beyond that boundary, the suffix effectively disappears.

Layer 2 — The Celtic West

Ker-, Plou-, Lan-: the Breton names that refused to become French

234 communes
95% in core Brittany

Ker- means "village" or "house" in Breton. Plou- (from Latin plebs) was the Breton word for a parish. Lan- means "hermitage" or holy enclosure. Together they appear in 234 commune names — 95% of which are in the three departments of Côtes-d'Armor, Finistère, and Morbihan.

Breton is a Celtic language, the closest surviving relative of the ancient Gaulish spoken across Gaul before Rome arrived. It was brought to Armorica (Brittany) by migrants from Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries CE — roughly contemporary with the Anglo-Saxon settlement of England — and has remained there ever since. Around 200,000 people still speak it today, mostly in Finistère.

The Revolution tried to standardise commune names into French and largely succeeded. Brittany's Ker- and Plou- names survived because there was no French equivalent that carried the same local meaning. Kerbors, Lannion, Plougastel-Daoulas remain as Celtic islands in an overwhelmingly Latin-derived landscape.

Layer 3 — The Occitan Southwest

-ac: the suffix that gives us Cognac, Bergerac, and 870 other names

872 communes
27% in Gironde, Dordogne, Charente

The suffix -ac derives from the Latin -acum, meaning "estate of" or "property of." Roman landowners gave their names to their estates: Cognacius became Cognac, Bergiracus became Bergerac. When Latin evolved into Occitan — the language of the medieval south — the suffix became productive across the whole region, applied to hundreds of new settlements.

The 872 -ac communes form a dense belt across the southwest: Gironde (89 communes, 10%), Dordogne (78, 9%), Charente (69, 8%). The pattern thins rapidly north of the Loire, which historically marked the linguistic boundary between the langue d'oïl (northern French) and the langue d'oc (Occitan). North of the Loire, people said oïl for "yes"; south of it, oc. The names know where they are.

Cognac and Bergerac are the most famous -ac names in the world. Both are in the Charente/Dordogne corridor, both give their names to beverages with global reach, and both carry the ghost of a Roman landowner who has been dead for 1,500 years.

Layer 4 — The Frankish North

-court: the Frankish naming habit, still visible in Picardy

698 communes
32% in Somme + Pas-de-Calais + Aisne

The suffix -court comes from the Frankish word for a farm or enclosed courtyard (hof, later curia in Latin). Frankish settlers — Germanic tribes who moved into northern Gaul from the Rhine after the fall of Rome — named their estates after their leaders: Bellicourt (Bellicus's farm), Aguilcourt, Haraucourt. The naming convention was systematic enough that its geographic footprint is still visible today.

Of 698 -court communes, the highest concentrations are in the Somme (95 communes, 14%), Pas-de-Calais (62, 9%), Oise (53, 8%), and Aisne (52, 7%) — a tight band across Picardy and Artois, exactly where the Frankish settlement was most intense in the 5th and 6th centuries CE. The Franks gave their name to France itself; -court is the signature they left in the northern soil.

The pattern also appears in the Vosges (63 communes, 9%) and eastward into Lorraine — a reminder that the Frankish zone extended east as well as north, following the rivers and Roman roads that carried settlers into the former Roman province of Belgica.

Layer 5 — The -ville Landscape

-ville: from Roman villa to France's most common place-name suffix

949 communes
16% in Seine-Maritime alone

The suffix -ville is everywhere, but it is not evenly distributed. Of 949 communes ending in -ville, 156 (16%) are in Seine-Maritime — the heart of Normandy. The next-highest departments are Eure (84) and Manche (51), both also in Normandy. The three departments together account for 30% of all -ville communes in France.

The Latin villa (farmstead, estate) was the basic unit of Roman rural settlement. When Norse raiders settled Normandy in 911 CE under Rollo, they adopted the local Latin-derived naming convention — Trouville, Deauville, Bénouville, Hérouville — rather than importing Germanic suffixes. The result is an extraordinary concentration of -ville names across the old Duchy of Normandy, making the Seine-Maritime the most -ville-dense département in France by a wide margin.

The suffix spread beyond Normandy through French linguistic influence — it appears in Lorraine, the Alps, and elsewhere — but nowhere in France does it cluster as densely as in the Seine estuary, where Viking chieftains were naming their new estates in a language borrowed from the people they had just conquered.

The layer above all layers: 3,879 saints

Each of the five layers above encodes a specific period, language, and region. One pattern cuts across all of them: the Saint- prefix, which appears in 3,879 communes — 11.1% of all of France. It is not regionally concentrated; every corner of the country has them. It is the Christian layer, applied over everything else between the 5th and 15th centuries, when parishes took the names of their patron saints.

The Revolution stripped the saints from street names and invented secular alternatives: Saint-Tropez became Héraclée, Saint-Malo became Port-Malo. Within a decade, most had taken their saints back. Secular France is built on top of a saintly substrate, and the commune names make it visible.

Reading the sediment

The five layers don't sort neatly by date. The Occitan -ac names came from Roman landowners in the 1st century CE. The Breton Ker- and Plou- names came from Celtic migrants in the 5th century. The Frankish -court names came from Germanic settlers in the 5th and 6th centuries. The Norman -ville concentration in Seine-Maritime came from Norse settlers in the 10th century. The Alsatian -heim names are the longest-running experiment: they predate France entirely and have survived its every attempt at linguistic homogenisation.

Together they cover about 10% of France's communes — around 3,125 place names that are, in the strictest sense, not French at all. They are Gaulish, Breton, Occitan, Frankish, German. France absorbed the territories, standardised the administration, and made everyone carry identity documents in French. It never quite got around to renaming the villages.
