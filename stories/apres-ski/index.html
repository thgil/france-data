Après-ski: France's secret bar capitals

France has 49,385 bars. The communes with the most per resident aren't in Bretagne or Nord. They're in the Alps.

2026-05-06 4 min read

The most-cited bar fact about France is the absolute count: 49,385 active débits de boissons, Nord and Pas-de-Calais together edging Paris, Finistère punching above its weight per capita. All true. But those are raw numbers or rough département averages. Ask a sharper question — which commune has the most bars per resident? — and the answer is nothing you'd expect.

It's Les Deux Alpes, an Alpine ski resort in Isère. Population: 1,933. Bars: 39. That's 201 bars per 10,000 residents — 23 times the national average of 8.8. The next four spots are also ski stations: Morzine (191/10k), Orcières (161/10k), Risoul (151/10k), Châtel (134/10k).

Why ski resorts top the list

The answer is hidden in the denominator. Bars per resident uses the permanent population — the 1,933 people who actually live in Les Deux Alpes year-round. But the resort's 39 bars don't serve 1,933 people: they serve the hundreds of thousands of skiers who pass through each winter. The metric conflates resident infrastructure with tourist infrastructure, and ski stations are extreme cases of the mismatch.

It's the same logic behind Paris having 14.6 bars per 10,000 residents — twice the national average — while the city's actual daytime population (offices, tourists, commuters) is three or four times the census count. Dense places with high footfall always look bar-rich on a per-resident basis.

The non-ski exceptions

Five of the top 25 communes break the ski pattern. Rogliano (Haute-Corse, 129/10k) is a hilltop village at the tip of Cap Corse — a place where the bar-as-village-centre is intact and the permanent population small. La Roche-Bernard (Morbihan, 115/10k) and Île-aux-Moines (Morbihan, 111/10k) carry the Breton drinking tradition into the ranking. Saint-Tropez (Var, 95/10k) is the coastal tourism equivalent of a ski station: a summer resort town whose 34 bars serve far more visitors than the 3,578 census residents. Deauville (Calvados, 89/10k) does the same on the Normandy coast.

The pattern is consistent: a small permanent population, a large seasonal or tourist flow, and bars licensed to serve the visitors.

The genuine drinking heartlands

At département level, the ski effect averages out. Savoie (16.0/10k) and Haute-Savoie appear, but they're not at the top. The actual leaders are France's most remote, rural départements: Lozère (25.7/10k), Haute-Corse (23.9/10k), Cantal (22.6/10k), Creuse (21.3/10k). These are places where the village café never quite died — where a population of 50,000–160,000 maintains a bar density that no IDF suburb approaches.

The bar desert

Zoom back out: 66.4% of French communes — 23,253 out of 35,014 — have no registered bar at all. That's a higher share than the medical desert (36.6% of communes). Of those that do have a bar, 6,191 have exactly one: the last café de France, the place where the postman has coffee and the farmers argue about the weather.

How many bars does a commune have?

The ski resort story and the bar-desert story are two faces of the same phenomenon. Bars follow people — and in France, people are profoundly unevenly distributed. The mountains and the coasts attract transient crowds large enough to sustain dozens of bars per village. The empty interior of France — the diagonale du vide — has lost its bars along with its people.

A note on the data. Bar counts come from INSEE's SIRENE business registry (active establishments, NAF code 56.30Z — débits de boissons), geocoded to commune level. Population figures are from the DREES APL 2023 dataset, which uses an INSEE 2021 population base (34,886 communes with valid population data). Communes with null or zero population in that dataset are excluded from per-capita calculations. The minimum population filter of 500 removes communes where a handful of bars and a tiny resident population produce extreme but uninterpretable ratios.
