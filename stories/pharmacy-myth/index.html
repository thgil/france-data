Every pharmacy in Île-de-France

Three maps, three angles on what French neighbourhood density actually looks like — and a couple of myths corrected along the way.

2026-04-16 6 min read

Two myths about French pharmacies travel further than they deserve. The first is that France has more pharmacies than bakeries — a claim that sounds plausible because the green crosses are everywhere, and because you know how many boulangeries there are. But nationally there are roughly 21,000 pharmacies and roughly 35,000 bakeries; bakeries outnumber pharmacies by about 1.7 to 1. The second myth is subtler: that France leads Europe in pharmacy density. It doesn't. Greece runs at 97 pharmacies per 100,000 residents; France sits at 33, just above the OECD average of 28. Both myths flatly contradict the OECD's own published figures (Health at a Glance 2023, 2021 data). Better to look at the map than argue the count.

The Région Île-de-France open data portal publishes 3,991 individual pharmacy points — FINESS-registered, each with name, address, latitude/longitude, and an ouverture date — plus the INSEE Base Permanente des Équipements commune-level counts that include matching boulangerie figures for the same 1,300 IDF communes. Three maps follow, built from those two sources.

Where the network actually is

The IDF pharmacy network didn't appear in one go. The earliest ouverture date in the dataset is 1943-12-27; the most recent in our snapshot is 2013-10-15. Watch the dots arrive in chronological order and the geography of the build-out becomes visible: dense inner-city cores filling first, then a ring of suburban nodes, then the outer communes catching up decades later.

One caveat on the dateouv field: in French regulatory practice the ouverture date can reset when a pharmacy changes ownership or transfers its licence. Early dates therefore undercount, and recent years oversample slightly. The shape of the curve is real; year-by-year absolute counts are noisier than they look.

Look at the resulting distribution and a quieter pattern surfaces. Pull back to commune level, switch to density rather than raw counts, and the same neighbourhoods top both the pharmacy and the bakery lists.

Pharmacies and bread don't compete

Pharmacies don't crowd out boulangeries — they move in alongside them. The same arrondissements (the 8e, 9e, 5e) top both lists in roughly the same order; the same outer communes sit near the bottom of both. French law forbids chain ownership of pharmacies: each officine must be majority-owned by a licensed pharmacist, and new licences are gated to local population thresholds — one per 2,500 residents in communes under 30,000, one per 4,500 in larger ones. Bakeries follow the same urban logic by accident — independent ownership, neighbourhood scale, foot traffic. They cluster on the same commercial streets because they answer to the same pressures.

Standing in the picture

Looking at the city from above is one thing. Standing inside it is another. Click anywhere in IDF to see how many pharmacies and bakeries are within a five-, ten-, and fifteen-minute walk.

Both myths land softer when you've seen the geography. The pharmacy count is just a number; the network is the story. France doesn't have the most pharmacies per capita, or more pharmacies than bakeries — it has a particular kind of small-shop, regulated, neighbourhood-scale infrastructure, replicated across thousands of corners. The unusual thing isn't the count. It's the urban form.

A note on the data. Commune-level counts of pharmacies and bakeries come from INSEE's Base Permanente des Équipements (BPE), republished by Région Île-de-France, vintage circa 2014, with 2010 census population. The individual pharmacy points come from the IDF "Carte des pharmacies" dataset (FINESS register). The BPE commune aggregate (4,228 pharmacies) and the FINESS point file (3,991) differ because they're different vintages and definitions — BPE includes some establishments FINESS classifies separately. The structural pattern (where the dense neighbourhoods are) is durable; the absolute counts have shifted since.

Density per 10,000 residents is computed from BPE counts and 2010 population. The Mauregard outlier (1 pharmacy / 314 residents = 63.7 per 10k) is a small-commune artefact — the rural pharmacy serves the surrounding villages but the population denominator is tiny. Density figures are unreliable for communes under ~1,000 residents; treat them as indicative.
