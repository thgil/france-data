Café déserts

The raw count puts Paris first. The per-capita story is stranger: ski resorts, rural Lozère, and a belt of 1970s new towns where the neighbourhood café simply never existed.

2026-05-06 5 min read

France has 49,385 registered bars. We mapped all of them. Paris led by total count, Bretagne punched above its weight, and the familiar diagonale du vide ran empty through the middle. But total count is a population story in disguise — a big département has more bars because it has more people. The more interesting question is: which commune has the most bars for its residents?

The answer, once you normalise for population, scrambles the leaderboard entirely.

Two-thirds of communes have zero bars

Before ranking the densest, consider the emptiest. Of France's 35,014 communes, 23,253 — two in three — have not a single registered débit de boissons. That's 11.3 million people living in a place with no licensed bar.

This isn't surprising in itself — France has thousands of tiny rural communes where the last café closed a generation ago. What's surprising is where the per-capita extremes actually fall.

The ski resort effect

The commune with the highest bar density in France — among those with a population of at least 500 — is Les Deux Alpes in Isère: 39 bars for 1,933 permanent residents. That's 201.8 bars per 10,000 people, or roughly one bar for every 50 residents. The national average is one for every 1,136.

The top 20 is dominated by ski stations and Corsican tourist villages. These are places where the registered business population — the number of bars serving an inflated tourist base — was captured at a moment when the permanent residential population was tiny. Every one of those bars stays registered year-round; only the customers leave in April.

Lozère beats Paris

Zoom out to the département level, smooth away the tourist outliers, and a different pattern emerges. The most bar-dense departments per capita are not the Côte d'Azur or Bretagne. They're the rural interior: Lozère (25.7 per 10k), Haute-Corse (23.9), Cantal (22.6), Creuse (21.3).

Paris — with 3,121 bars for 2.1 million residents — comes in at 14.6 per 10k, ranking 18th. Lozère, with 135 bars for 52,000 people, beats it by 76%. The diagonale du vide that looked so empty on the raw-count map is, per capita, one of France's most café-rich regions. The village café culture that Lozère and Cantal preserved — or simply never lost to urban attrition — shows up clearly in the numbers.

The starkest internal contrast is inside Île-de-France. Paris (14.6/10k) is well above average. But cross the périphérique: Hauts-de-Seine (3.3), Essonne (3.4), Yvelines (3.6). The same suburbs that form the ring of medical deserts around Paris are also bar-scarce. The geography of access — to doctors, to boulangeries, to neighbourhood cafés — follows the same arterial logic.

The new-town paradox

The lowest-density communes in France — among those big enough to expect at least one bar — are not isolated rural hamlets. They are planned cities from the 1970s grands projets. Tournefeuille (Toulouse métropole), Élancourt (Yvelines), Torcy (Marne-la-Vallée): population 20,000–30,000, exactly one registered bar each.

These are villes nouvelles — built around car access, supermarkets, and residential blocks, not around a central square with a tabac. The neighbourhood café was absent from the architectural plan, and half a century later the numbers confirm it was never retrofitted in.

The café is a piece of infrastructure — regulated, registered, countable — just like the pharmacy or the boulangerie. Its absence is as legible in the data as its presence. Two-thirds of French communes have none. But in Lozère, where there are barely 52,000 people, there are 135 cafés. The metrics of density, access, and absence keep tracing the same underlying geography — and it's not always the map you'd expect.

A note on the data. Bar counts come from INSEE SIRENE (NAF 56.30Z, active establishments, March 2026 snapshot) — the same dataset behind Story 4 (bars). Population figures are from the INSEE 2021 census as distributed in the DREES APL commune dataset. The per-capita analysis covers communes where both a SIRENE commune code and an APL population figure could be matched (11,749 communes with ≥ 1 bar; 36 bars from 12 unmatched codes excluded). Communes with no bars in SIRENE are not omitted — their zero count is included in département and national totals.
