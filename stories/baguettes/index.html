The Bread Gap

Île-de-France has 6,299 bakeries for 11.8 million people — but they are not distributed evenly. Some arrondissements have a boulangerie for every 480 residents. Two towns of five thousand have none.

2026-05-05 5 min read

France takes its bread seriously. The boulangerie is legally defined: to carry the name, a bakery must bake its own loaves on the premises, not just sell factory product. There are no franchise boulangeries, not really. Each one is an independent shop with its own oven and its own sourdough starter, serving a radius of a few streets.

In Île-de-France those streets are not all equal. The region's 1,300 communes share 6,299 bakeries. But nearly half those communes — 607 out of 1,300 — have zero. And even among the places that do have bread, the supply varies by a factor of more than three, depending on which arrondissement or suburb you call home.

The zero-bakery communes are mostly tiny rural villages in Seine-et-Marne where the nearest boulangerie is a short drive away. That number — 607 out of 1,300 — is stark but doesn't tell the urban story. Among communes with more than 5,000 residents, only two have no bakery at all: Saint-Germain-lès-Arpajon in Essonne (9,338 residents) and Nandy in Seine-et-Marne (5,814). They're genuine outliers.

The more revealing picture is density: how many bakeries per 10,000 residents does each commune have? That's where the gap shows up.

Where the bread is densest

Filtered for communes with at least 5,000 residents — enough to give density figures meaning — and ranked by bakeries per 10,000 residents, a clear pattern emerges. The chart below shows the top 30.

Paris 1er leads: 36 bakeries for 17,308 residents works out to one boulangerie for every 481 people. That is genuinely exceptional. Most of Europe's major cities sit between 2 and 6 bakeries per 10,000; the 1er is above 20.

But look at who breaks into the top ten from outside Paris. Enghien-les-Bains — a lakeside spa town in Val-d'Oise — has 11.9 bakeries per 10,000, putting it ahead of the Paris 3e, 7e, and every arrondissement south of the Seine. Le Raincy, a dense working-class suburb in Seine-Saint-Denis, comes in at 11.6 — ahead of sixteen of the twenty Paris arrondissements. Fontainebleau, the forest-and-château town at the southern edge of Seine-et-Marne, sits at 11.2. These are not obvious places to look for bread density.

Among communes with more than 5,000 residents, the three best-served spots outside Paris are all different in character: a genteel spa resort (Enghien-les-Bains), a dense working-class banlieue (Le Raincy), and a historic market town (Fontainebleau). Bread density tracks foot traffic more than wealth.

Paris, arrondissement by arrondissement

Within Paris, the gradient is steep. The 1er arrondissement's 20.8 bakeries per 10,000 is 3.5 times higher than the 13e's 5.7. The wealthier western arrondissements (6e, 7e, 8e) don't top the list — the 1er and 4e, dense commercial and tourist districts, do. The outer ring (13e through 20e) forms a consistent lower tier.

The pattern tracks footfall more than income. The 1er is the Louvre, Les Halles, and the Palais-Royal — millions of tourists and commuters passing through per year. The 15e, which ranks last among Paris arrondissements at 6.2 per 10k despite being the most populous, is a largely residential district. The bread supply follows the crowd.

The département gap

Step back to the regional level, and the contrast between Paris and its suburbs becomes stark. Paris the département (the city proper) averages 8.0 bakeries per 10,000 — nearly double the rate of Essonne at the bottom (4.3). Seine-Saint-Denis, often described as deprived, does better than the wealthy western suburbs of Hauts-de-Seine.

This last chart reframes the geography of French bread. The metric that feels like a cultural universal — access to a boulangerie — is in practice a function of urban density and foot traffic. The most walkable, commercially active places have the most boulangeries. The sprawling outer suburbs, built for cars and commuters rather than strolling and street-level retail, have the fewest.

A commune like Saint-Germain-lès-Arpajon — a post-war residential suburb, car-oriented, with nine thousand residents and no bakery — isn't a cultural anomaly. It's a planning outcome. The bread gap, like so many gaps in Île-de-France, is an urbanisme story dressed up as a food story.
